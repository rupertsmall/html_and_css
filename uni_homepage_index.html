Probability Distributions

Many things are completely random, so that rather than being able to predict a specific outcome, its only possible to determine which outcomes appear to occur more often, or less often. If you take alot of measurements, then your knowledge can still become quite accurate, even though the outcomes are random. By taking many measurements you can begin to draw a precise picture of which outcomes occur with a certain probability. The resulting picture is a probability distribution. For example if I count the number of ninjas I see on my way home I might notice that on some days its 16 ninjas while on others its only 2 ninjas. If I make careful notes of the number of ninjas I see every day, then over time I might be able to draw a graph like this



which tells me the probability that I meet a certain number of ninjas on any given day. So you can see that in my particular part of the country its highly likely that I'll see about 12 ninjas on my way home, but very unlikely I'll see a very low number of ninjas (e.g. 0 ninjas) or a very high number of ninjas (e.g. 25 ninjas).

Random Matrix Theory

Matrices are just arrays of numbers, organised nice and neat into a little box. Each number in the box will have a certain value, but if the value is random, then we call the matrix a Random Matrix. Since matrices can describe the outcome of physical measurements they are used by physicists and mathematicians - alot! An example of a \(3\times 3\) matrix is $$\left( \begin{array}{ccc} 11 & 3 & 7 \\ 2 & 0 & 5 \\ 5 & 2 & 9 \end{array} \right)$$ There are many ways to create a Random Matrix. To create a random \(3\times 3\) matrix for example, you could roll a dice 9 times and put the result of each roll into one of the entries of the matrix. If you rolled the dice alot and created a whole jungle full of \(3\times 3\) random matrices, then you would have created whats described as an \(ensemble\) of random matrices.

Particle Diagrams

There is a very beautiful connection between Random Matrices and particles. The connection comes about because groups of particles can interact in a very random fashion. When they do this the measurements that physicists make often look random. Taking lots of measurements sometimes allows them to discover patterns in the randomness, such as which kinds of interactions occur with higher probability and which kinds occur with lower probability. The result of all these measurements is that physicists can draw a probability distribution describing their measurements. To come up with theoretical predictions for their measurements its important to become really good at counting lots of particles, and counting the number of ways groups of particles can interact. Thats where particle diagrams come in. I let the diagram $$ \mu~ \_\_\_\_\_\_ ~ \nu$$ tell me that the sets \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) (which each have \(m\) single-particle states inside) share at least \((m-k)\) single-particle states, and I let the diagram $$ \rho~ --- ~ \sigma$$ tell me that the sets \(\rho\) and \(\sigma\) share at least \(k\) single-particle states. Now if the total set of all possible single-particle states is of size \(l\) then I can label all of them in a nice sequence \(1, 2, 3, \ldots, l\) and the number of ways I can choose a set \(\mu\) with \(m\) unique single-particle states inside is just $$l\choose m $$ But this is just the beginning! I can ask lots of questions about the number of possible combinations for certain particle diagrams, such as how many states satisfy the diagram $$ \mu~ \_\_\_\_\_\_ ~ \nu$$ Well, there are \(l\choose m\) ways to choose an \(m\)-set like \(\mu\) and since \(\nu\) must share \((m-k)\) of these we have the choice of \({{m}\choose{m-k}}\) different ways to take \((m-k)\) single-particle states from \(\mu\) to put into \(\nu\). Then, since \(\nu\) is made up of \(m\) single-particle states and we have only chosen \((m-k)\) of them so far, we can choose a further \(k\) unique states to put into \(\nu\) and there are \({{l - (m-k)}\choose{k}}\) ways of doing that. So the total number of ways to make the diagram \(\mu~ \_\_\_\_\_\_ ~ \nu \) is in fact $$ {l\choose m}{{m}\choose{m-k}}{{l - (m-k)}\choose{k}} $$ How about something even fancier? The diagram $$ \mu~ \_\_\_\_\_\_ ~ \nu ~ --- ~ \sigma$$ is surely alot fancier. So how many ways are there to make this diagram? Again, there are \(l\choose m\) ways to choose an \(m\)-set like \(\mu\) and \({{m}\choose{m-k}}{{l - (m-k)}\choose{k}}\) ways to choose the corresponding \(\nu\) (as we saw with the first example). Now \(\sigma\) must share at least \(k\) of the single-particle states that are in \(\nu\) and there are \({m\choose k}\) ways to select them. Then, since \(\sigma\) is made up of \(m\) single-particle states and we have only chosen \(k\) of them so far, we can choose a further \((m-k)\) unique states to put into \(\sigma\) and there are \({{l - k}\choose{m-k}}\) ways of doing that. So the total number of ways to make the diagram \(\mu~ \_\_\_\_\_\_ ~ \nu ~ --- ~ \sigma\) is in fact $$ {l\choose m}{{m}\choose{m-k}}{{l - (m-k)}\choose{k}}{m\choose k}{{l - k}\choose{m-k}} $$ So you can see that even for quite simple diagrams the expression is quite big. When using the diagrams to count combinations in Random Matrix Theory the diagrams are much bigger, and many are 3 dimensional! Still, if you have understood this far you have understood the fundamentals of particle diagrams and how they are used to count the number of combinations of certain relationships between groups of particles.
